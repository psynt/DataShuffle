Section B. General Information

Educational Aims
 
 
Students completing the BSc (Hons) in Computer Science will
  • Acquire a general knowledge and understanding of computers and computer science
  • Develop a more specialised knowledge of the theoretical foundation of computer science and other selected topics within the discipline
  • Encounter and work with a variety of approaches, techniques and tools in order to solve the different types of problems encountered in the discipline
  • Acquire the ability to communicate effectively orally and in writing both under pressure and reflectively
  • Understand the professional, legal and ethical aspects of the discipline and know how to act on these appropriately.

    Outline Description of Course
     
     
    Under the provision of the Nottingham/UNMC Mobility Programme Part I Honours students may pursue their studies at the University's Malaysian Campus for their second year.

     
    Distinguishing Features
     
     

    The Computer Science course combines the theoretical foundations of the discipline with practical applications of computer systems. Throughout the course students study a range of modules that support these two themes, and the connection between them. From the theoretical side students learn about the mathematical models of computation that underpin the discipline. On the practical side students learn how to design, program, and construct software, moving from small- to large-scale systems. These themes converge in the study of the realisation of the theoretical models in modern computer systems.

    Extensive project and practical work, including a software engineering group project in Part I and an individual dissertation in Part II, support these themes, and allow students to further develop their abilities. The projects also encourage students to further develop their time management, group interaction, and presentation skills. Group and individual projects are conducted in collaboration with a supervisor who offers expert advice and support through regular meetings. Project topics in Computer Science generally aim to combine theoretical foundations and practical applications within a specific area of research.

    Finally, students can choose from a wide range of options in Computer Science, and outside of the School, to explore topics of personal interest in support of the core Computer Science material. More detailed information on the content of modules, the range of modules to be taken, and availability in the current year, can be found in the Undergraduate Module Catalogue and the School's Undergraduate information website.

    Progression Information:

    This course is covered by the University Regulations of assessment, and the following information should be read in the context of the relevant University Regulations for first degrees.

    The pass mark for all modules is 40%, and in the qualifying year G51PGA (Programming and Algorithms) is non-compensatable. Students who fail to progress under the University Regulations, or who fail G51PGA, may be offered the opportunity to transfer to the BSc (Ord) degree.

    Degree Information:

    Degrees are awarded based on the candidate's performance in Parts I and II. The marks from Part I are weighted 40% and those from Part II are weighted 60%. The weighted average is interpreted as a degree classification as follows:

    Weighted Average Degree classification
    70-100% First class
    60-69% Second class, division I
    50-59% Second class, division II
    40-49% Third class
    0-39% Fail

    Candidates whose final classification falls on a borderline will be considered at the Examiners' Meeting for the award of the next degree classification. This decision will be based on the candidate's profile of marks over their course of study in consultation with the External Examiners. Borderline marks are as follows:

    Weighted Average Borderline for
    68% First class
    59% Second class, division I
    49% Second class, division II
    39% Third class

    In the case of borderlines, in the absence of (formal) extenuating circumstances, a student should be given the higher class if any of the three following criteria are met:

  • Half or more of the final stage credits are in the higher class;
  • Half or more of the final and penultimate stage credits are in the higher class;
  • Half or more of the credits in all stages which count towards the degree classification are in the higher class.

    • Candidates whose performance does not merit the award of an Honours degree will be considered by the board of examiners for the award of a Pass or Ordinary degree.

    Course Weightings %
    Part I: 40.0
    Part II: 60.0
    Degree Calculation Model: Arithmetic Mean

    Section D. Learning Outcomes
     
     
    The following learning outcomes refer to the BSc (Hons) course. Students who transfer to the BSc (Ord) course will be expected to meet most, but not all, of these outcomes, and will generally require a lower level of achievement.
    Knowledge and Understanding
    Graduates should demonstrate knowledge and understanding of:
  • The theory of programming;
  • The practice of programming;
  • The strengths and weaknesses of computer tools, applications and other resources;
  • Applied mathematics and formal methods in the computer science context;
  • The synergy of hardware and software in computer systems implementation;
  • The properties of networked and distributed systems.
    • Intellectual Skills
    Graduates should have the ability to
  • Apply and deploy mathematical ability, practices and tools
  • Understand and logically evaluate requirements and specifications
  • Think independently while giving due weight to the arguments of others
  • Understand complex ideas and relate them to specific problems or questions
  • Analyse systematically and effectively, substantial quantities of information
    • Professional/Practical Skills
    Graduates should have the skills required to
  • Program in various paradigms
  • Comprehend and apply software engineering methodologies
  • Evaluate available tools, applications, algorithms and data structures, and select those that are fit for purpose within a given domain
  • Work effectively and ethically with others
  • Extend their own computing-related abilities by specialising or generalising from a personal skills base
    • Transferable/Key Skills
    Graduates should have the ability to
  • Solve problems
  • Utilise mathematics
  • Work productively in a collaborative environment
  • Communicate effectively in writing and orally
  • Organise and manage their own (and other's time) including scheduling of tasks and meeting deadlines
  • Retrieve information from appropriate sources (e.g. learning resource centre, internet etc)
  • Reflect upon and assess their own progress, strengths and weaknesses
    • Teaching and Learning for all sections if summarised
    In the initial stages of the course most of the teaching and learning takes place in lectures, supported by extensive tutorial and laboratory classes. As students progress the role of project work increases, and tutorials become less common as students are encouraged to take more responsibility for their own learning. Coursework, commonly taking the form of programming tasks supported by laboratory classes, allows students to develop their skills and knowledge through practical experience.
    Assessment for all sections if summarised
    Assessment is through a mixture of formal examinations and coursework, including written and oral reports, programming tasks, and design scenarios. Project work is important in the later stages of the degree, and is assessed through reports, presentations, and demonstrations.
